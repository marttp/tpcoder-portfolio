There's a conversation that keeps coming up every few months, and it usually sounds like this: *"Do I still need to study fundamentals? How much do I actually need to know before I can start?"*

This post tries to answer that directly — not the version where you need to read seven books before your first job, and not the version where fundamentals don't matter because you have Claude Code or a coding agent doing it for you. Both of those are wrong.

What I'm going to do is go through each area — data structures, networking, databases, operating systems, system design — and separate what you need to **start** from what you need to **grow**. Those are different lists, and conflating them is what makes people either underprepare or burn out trying to reach a bar that isn't actually the entry-level bar.

---

## Why the Question Is Being Asked Wrong

Most people frame this as: *"Do I need fundamentals to get a job?"*

That's too narrow. The better question is: **"Do I need fundamentals to actually understand what my code does?"**

And the answer is yes — because understanding is what separates engineers who can debug from engineers who can only rewrite, who can diagnose from who can only guess, who grow steadily from who plateau after two years.

Here's what I've observed in engineers who skipped the fundamentals:

- They ship code that works at 100 rows and breaks at 100,000 — because they didn't understand complexity or indexes
- They can't read infrastructure-layer errors — a 502 vs 504 tells you completely different things, but only if you know what sits between your service and the client
- They can add features but can't diagnose slowdowns — no mental model for where the bottleneck actually lives
- They hit an unfamiliar bug with no frame of reference, so they rewrite instead of fix

This isn't about interview trivia. It's about being able to function when things go wrong — and in production systems, they will.

In the current environment, this gap becomes more visible. When AI generates code, you're reviewing it. Reviewing requires knowing what *correct* looks like — not just what *plausible* looks like. AI reliably produces plausible. Fundamentals are what help you tell the difference.

## The Actual Entry-Level Floor

Before walking through each area in depth, let me answer the question directly: *what do you need to know to start your first job?*

This is the honest minimum — not the ideal, not what you'll have after two years, just the floor:

- **Data structures**: Know arrays and hash maps. Understand why you'd pick one over the other. Have a rough sense that O(n) is slower than O(1). That's it.
- **Networking**: Know what HTTP is. Understand GET, POST, PUT, DELETE at a conceptual level. Know what 200, 400, 404, and 500 mean. Know that HTTPS means the connection is encrypted.
- **Databases**: Write basic queries — SELECT, INSERT, UPDATE, DELETE. Know that indexes exist and make reads faster. Understand what a transaction is at a conceptual level (either it all works, or none of it does).
- **Operating systems**: Know that processes and threads are different things. That's enough for day one.
- **System design**: Know what a load balancer is. Know that caches exist and what problem they solve.
- **Design diagrams**: Be able to read a simple class diagram and understand the basic relationships. Know what a sequence diagram shows. Know what an ER diagram represents.

Everything in the rest of this article beyond that list? That's what the first two years are for building. Not a checklist to clear before you're allowed to apply.

### Why it feels like the bar has shifted

It has — but not in the way most people think.

The actual floor for a first job hasn't changed dramatically. What has changed is the **ceiling**. What's expected at mid-level is higher now, and companies want early-career engineers who can ramp up quickly. That ceiling pressure gets projected backward, and suddenly the expectation described for "early career" reads like what used to be the two-year mark.

So when someone says "the bar is higher now," they're usually right about mid-level expectations and slightly wrong about entry-level. The floor is still achievable. What's different is the velocity expected after you start — how fast you're expected to move from the floor toward that higher ceiling.

That's actually the useful reframe. It's not that you need to know more before starting. It's that you need to have a clear direction for what to learn once you're in the role. The sections below are that direction.

## Data Structures & Algorithms — The Evaluation Layer

Let me be clear upfront: I am **not** talking about LeetCode grind. I'm talking about understanding data structures well enough to know when the wrong one was used.

![Data structure visual metaphors](/assets/images/blogs/fundamentals-2026/data-structures-metaphors.png)

### What you actually need to know

**Arrays and dynamic arrays** — the default structure everyone reaches for. Understand why random access is O(1) but insertion in the middle is O(n). This comes up constantly in code that repeatedly inserts into the front of a list inside a loop.

**Hash maps** — probably the structure you'll use most. Understand that lookup is O(1) average, not always. Understand hash collisions, load factor, and why a HashMap is not sorted. Know when a LinkedHashMap or TreeMap matters.

**Linked lists** — less commonly used directly, but essential for understanding queues, LRU caches, and how memory allocation works under the hood. Also, understanding pointer-style thinking helps with tree traversal.

**Trees** — BST, heap, trie. The heap matters for priority queues. The trie matters for prefix search. BST matters for sorted data operations. You don't need to implement them from scratch — you need to recognize when to reach for them.

**Graphs** — BFS and DFS are not interview tricks. They come up in dependency resolution, service mesh traversal, recommendation systems, and any system with relationships. Understanding adjacency list vs matrix matters when the graph is sparse (most real-world graphs are).

**Stacks and queues** — know the difference, and know how they're used in call stacks, undo systems, task queues, and parsing.

### Complexity — the practical part

Time and space complexity are **not** about memorizing formulas. They're about being able to look at code and say: *"This is going to fall apart at scale."*

Nested loops that could be hash maps are a common source of O(n²) in real code — easy to miss if you can't read complexity.

### What you can skip

You do not need to know red-black tree rotations. You do not need to implement Dijkstra from memory. You do not need to solve dynamic programming puzzles under pressure. These are interview gatekeeping tools, not daily engineering skills. Learn the concept behind dynamic programming (memoization, overlapping subproblems) — but you don't need to be a DP wizard at early career.

## Networking — The Invisible Foundation

Networking is the area where I see the biggest gap in early and mid-level engineers. Everyone knows HTTP is "the web thing." Almost nobody understands it well enough to debug it.

### HTTP from the ground up

You need to understand HTTP not as a protocol you use, but as a protocol you read when things go wrong.

**Methods matter.** GET is idempotent and should not change state. POST is not. PUT replaces. PATCH modifies. DELETE removes. These are not conventions — they have semantic meaning that matters for caching, retries, and API design. Mixing up PUT and PATCH, or using GET for requests with a body, causes subtle issues that surface later in unexpected places.

**Status codes are a vocabulary.** 200 is success. 201 is created. 204 is success with no body. 400 is your fault. 401 is unauthenticated. 403 is unauthorized. 404 is not found. 409 is conflict. 422 is validation failure. 500 is server error. 502 is bad gateway (upstream failed). 503 is service unavailable. 504 is gateway timeout (upstream didn't respond in time).

Know the difference between 502 and 504. Know the difference between 401 and 403. They tell you different things about where to look when debugging.

![HTTP status code reference](/assets/images/blogs/fundamentals-2026/http-status-codes.png)

**Headers are the metadata layer.** `Content-Type`, `Authorization`, `Cache-Control`, `Accept`, `X-Request-ID`, `CORS` headers — you will interact with all of these. Understand that `Authorization: Bearer <token>` is a convention, not magic. Understand that `Cache-Control: no-store` prevents caching at every layer. Understand that CORS errors are a browser enforcement, not a network error.

### TCP — why it matters even if you never touch sockets

Most engineers treat TCP as a given. It handles delivery, done. But TCP has implications you feel every day:

- **Connection establishment is expensive.** Three-way handshake. This is why connection pooling exists in database clients and HTTP clients. Skipping it is a common shortcut that causes latency problems at scale.
- **TCP is stateful.** Connections exist between two endpoints. This is why horizontal scaling of stateful services is hard.
- **Timeouts are a TCP concern.** Connect timeout vs read timeout vs write timeout — these are different things. Using a single generic timeout without distinguishing them is a common oversight.

### DNS — debugging the resolution chain

DNS failures are common and confusing if you don't understand the resolution chain. Know that:

- DNS is cached at multiple levels: browser, OS, resolver
- TTL (time-to-live) controls how long cached entries are valid
- When you change DNS records, propagation takes time because of TTL
- In containerized environments, DNS resolution behaves differently — internal service names resolve through a different path than public internet names

When a service says "cannot resolve hostname," it's a DNS problem. When it says "connection refused," the service is down or not listening. When it says "connection timeout," the host is reachable but not responding. These are different failure modes that require different fixes.

### TLS / HTTPS

TLS is not optional in 2026. You need to understand:

- Certificate chains: leaf, intermediate, root CA
- Why certificate expiry causes incidents (and how to monitor it)
- The difference between TLS termination at the load balancer vs end-to-end TLS
- mTLS (mutual TLS) — you'll encounter this in service meshes and microservice architectures

You don't need to implement TLS. You need to understand it well enough to debug certificate errors and make decisions about where to terminate TLS in your architecture.

## Databases — Where Most Apps Actually Break

I'll be direct: **most production incidents I've seen trace back to a database problem.** Either a missing index, a bad query, a transaction deadlock, or a misunderstood consistency model. Databases are where fundamentals matter most for practical, daily backend work.

### Indexes — the most misunderstood performance tool

An index trades write performance for read performance. This is the fundamental trade-off, and it's one that's easy to overlook when reviewing schema changes or migration files.

**What you need to understand:**

**B-tree indexes** are the default in most relational databases. They support range queries, equality, and prefix matching. They're ordered. They work well for columns with high cardinality.

**Composite indexes** are indexes on multiple columns. Column order matters. An index on `(user_id, created_at)` can satisfy queries filtering on `user_id` alone, or filtering on both — but not queries filtering on `created_at` alone.

**Covering indexes** include all the columns needed for a query, so the database doesn't need to touch the actual table. This is a significant optimization for read-heavy queries.

**Index selectivity** is the ratio of distinct values to total rows. An index on a boolean column (two possible values) is almost useless. An index on a UUID column (near-unique values) is highly effective.

When reviewing schema changes or migration files, your first question should be: *"Which columns are in WHERE clauses, JOIN conditions, and ORDER BY? Are those indexed?"*

### Transactions and ACID

**Atomicity**: all operations in a transaction succeed, or none do. This is what prevents partial updates — if you update a user's balance and create a transaction record, you don't want one to succeed without the other.

**Consistency**: the database moves from one valid state to another. Constraints (foreign keys, unique constraints, check constraints) are enforced.

**Isolation**: concurrent transactions don't interfere with each other. This is the complicated one.

**Durability**: committed transactions survive crashes. This is about write-ahead logging and flushing to disk.

Isolation levels are where most engineers have gaps:

- **Read Uncommitted**: you can read changes from uncommitted transactions. Almost never what you want.
- **Read Committed**: you only read committed data, but you can read different values across two reads in the same transaction (non-repeatable read).
- **Repeatable Read**: the same query returns the same results within a transaction. Default in MySQL/InnoDB.
- **Serializable**: transactions behave as if they ran sequentially. Safest, but slowest.

Most applications run fine on Read Committed. But if you're implementing financial logic, inventory reservation, or any "check then act" pattern, you need to think carefully about isolation level — and it's easy to skip that thinking entirely.

### The N+1 Problem — The Most Common Performance Bug

This is the most common performance issue I run into, and it comes from not understanding how queries compose.

```
// AI generates this:
const users = await User.findAll();
for (const user of users) {
  const orders = await Order.findAll({ where: { userId: user.id } });
  // 1 query for users + N queries for orders = N+1 queries
}

// What it should be:
const users = await User.findAll({ include: [{ model: Order }] });
// 1 query with JOIN = 1 query total
```

If you don't know what N+1 is, you won't recognize it when you see it. And it will work perfectly in development with 10 users, then destroy performance in production with 10,000.

### Query Planning — reading EXPLAIN

`EXPLAIN` (or `EXPLAIN ANALYZE` in PostgreSQL) shows you how the database will execute a query. You need to know how to read it. The key signals:

- **Seq Scan**: sequential scan, reading every row. Sometimes correct for small tables, almost always wrong for large tables without a WHERE clause that uses an index.
- **Index Scan**: using an index. Usually good.
- **Index Only Scan**: reading from the index alone without touching the table. Best case for covered queries.
- **Hash Join / Nested Loop**: how tables are joined. Nested loop is expensive for large result sets.
- **rows**: estimated row count. Severely wrong estimates (1 vs 100,000) indicate stale statistics.

This is a skill you build over time, but knowing it exists and how to read it is something you should have by mid-level.

## Operating Systems & Concurrency — The Layer Below Your Code

Most application engineers treat the OS as a black box. That's fine up to a point. But there are concepts that leak through constantly, and missing them causes bugs that are very hard to debug.

### Processes vs threads vs async — they are not interchangeable

**Process**: an isolated execution environment with its own memory space. Processes don't share memory by default. Communication happens through IPC (pipes, sockets, shared memory). Spawning a process is expensive.

**Thread**: a unit of execution within a process. Threads share memory. This is what makes them fast and dangerous simultaneously — shared memory means race conditions.

**Async/coroutines**: cooperative multitasking within a single thread. No parallelism, but high concurrency for I/O-bound work. This is how Node.js works, how async/await works in Python, and how goroutines work in Go.

If your language/runtime is not clear in your head, you will write code that doesn't scale or has subtle data corruption bugs. Thread-unsafe access to shared state is easy to miss, especially in code that wasn't written with concurrency in mind.

### Race conditions and the basic patterns to prevent them

A race condition is when the outcome depends on the timing of concurrent operations. The classic example:

```
// Thread A and Thread B both read balance = 100
// Thread A adds 50: balance = 150, writes 150
// Thread B subtracts 30: balance = 70, writes 70
// Final balance: 70 — Thread A's write was lost
```

Solutions depend on the context:
- **Mutex/lock**: only one thread accesses the critical section at a time
- **Atomic operations**: hardware-level guarantees for simple operations
- **Immutability**: don't share mutable state — share copies or use message passing
- **Optimistic locking**: check-and-set with version numbers (common in databases)

You don't need to be a concurrency expert at early career. You need to know the danger signs and know when to ask: *"Is this shared state? Can two threads reach this simultaneously?"*

### Memory — stack vs heap, and why it matters

**Stack**: fixed-size, fast, automatically managed. Local variables live here. Stack overflow happens when you recurse too deeply (stack is full).

**Heap**: dynamic, larger, manually managed (or GC-managed). Objects, arrays, any dynamically allocated data lives here. Memory leaks happen here — when you hold references to objects you no longer need.

![Stack vs heap memory layout](/assets/images/blogs/fundamentals-2026/stack-vs-heap.png)

In garbage-collected languages (Java, Python, Go, JavaScript), you don't manually free memory. But you still cause memory leaks by holding references you don't need. Common causes:
- Unbounded caches
- Event listeners that are never removed
- Closures that capture large objects
- Static collections that grow indefinitely

Understanding that GC exists and why it occasionally pauses your application matters for latency-sensitive services. Understanding heap dumps and memory profiling is a mid-level skill worth having.

### File descriptors and connection limits

Every open file, socket, and pipe is a file descriptor. Operating systems have a limit on how many file descriptors a process can have open simultaneously. If you have a bug that opens connections and never closes them, you will hit this limit and your service will fail with a cryptic error.

This is why connection pooling is important in database clients. This is why HTTP clients should reuse connections. This is why you always close file handles after reading files.

## System Design — Not the Interview Kind

System design as taught in interview prep (draw boxes on a whiteboard, say "we need a cache, we need a message queue, done") is not what I mean. I mean understanding **what components actually do** and **what they fail to do**.

### Load balancers — what they actually do

A load balancer distributes incoming requests across multiple instances of your service. It also does:

- **Health checking**: removes instances that fail health checks from the pool
- **SSL termination**: handles TLS decryption so your service doesn't have to
- **Sticky sessions**: routes requests from the same client to the same instance (relevant for stateful services)

What it doesn't do: it doesn't make your service stateless. If your service stores session state in memory, load balancing across instances will cause problems — request 1 might build state on instance A, request 2 lands on instance B and the state is gone.

### Message queues — asynchrony and decoupling

A message queue (Kafka, RabbitMQ, SQS) decouples the producer from the consumer. The producer pushes to the queue and moves on. The consumer reads from the queue at its own pace.

What this solves:
- **Backpressure**: consumer can't keep up with producer — queue absorbs the burst
- **Reliability**: if consumer is down, messages wait in the queue instead of being lost
- **Decoupling**: producer and consumer don't need to be running simultaneously

What this doesn't solve:
- **Ordering guarantees**: queues don't always preserve order at scale — Kafka does per-partition, SQS does not guarantee it across shards
- **Exactly-once delivery**: most queues give you at-least-once, which means your consumer needs to handle duplicate messages (idempotency)
- **Transactions**: writing to a queue and writing to a database in the same atomic transaction is hard — look up the outbox pattern

### Caching — and why "add a cache" is never the complete answer

Caches trade consistency for performance. Every caching decision involves answering:

- **What's the invalidation strategy?** TTL (time-to-live), explicit invalidation, write-through, write-behind?
- **What happens on cache miss?** Does every miss hit the database? Thundering herd — when cache expires and 1,000 requests simultaneously hit the database.
- **What happens when the cache is down?** Does your service degrade gracefully or fail completely?
- **Is stale data acceptable?** For how long?

AI will suggest adding a cache and stop there. You need to complete the question.

### Failure modes — the questions you must ask

For any system component, ask:

1. What happens if this is slow? (timeout, retry logic)
2. What happens if this is down? (fallback, circuit breaker)
3. What happens if this returns incorrect data? (validation, consistency checks)
4. What happens if there are two of these? (consistency, coordination)

## Design Diagrams — Talking About Code With Your Team

There's a layer between knowing how to code and being able to communicate a design to your team. That layer is worth developing early — not because UML is a formal requirement, but because a shared visual language prevents a lot of misunderstanding in design discussions, code reviews, and architecture docs.

You don't need every diagram type. You don't need formal precision. You need enough consistency that when you draw a line between two boxes, everyone in the room means the same thing.

### Class diagrams — relationships between code

Class diagrams show how objects relate to each other: what owns what, what knows about what, what extends what. The notation doesn't need to be exact. The relationships do need to be clear.

The five relationships worth knowing:

- **Inheritance (is-a)**: hollow arrowhead pointing to the parent. `Dog` extends `Animal` — Dog is an Animal.
- **Composition (owns strongly)**: filled diamond on the owner. The child cannot exist without the parent. An `Order` owns its `LineItems` — delete the order, the line items go too.
- **Aggregation (has loosely)**: hollow diamond on the owner. The child exists independently. A `Team` has `Players` — a player can exist without that team.
- **Association**: plain arrow. Objects know about each other. A `User` places an `Order`.
- **Dependency**: dashed arrow. One class uses another temporarily. `OrderService` calls `PaymentGateway` to process a charge.

The practical use: when someone asks "should UserProfile be inside User, or separate?" — being able to sketch this and distinguish composition from aggregation makes the conversation much faster than explaining it in words. And the answer to that question has real consequences for database schema, deletion behavior, and API design.

### Sequence diagrams — message flow over time

You've already seen several in this article. Sequence diagrams show how services or components interact over time — which message goes where, in what order, and what comes back.

At entry level: be able to read one. At mid-level: be able to produce one to explain a flow you're designing or debugging. Most architecture discussions in backend teams eventually come down to "draw me a sequence diagram of what happens when X."

### ER diagrams — database schema at a glance

ER diagrams represent database structure: tables as entities, relationships as lines with cardinality. One user has many orders. One order has many line items. Many-to-many relationships need a junction table.

In practice most teams sketch informal ER diagrams rather than formal Crow's Foot notation. As long as it communicates the schema clearly, the formality level doesn't matter.

### The shared language point

The goal isn't certification-level accuracy. The goal is that your team reads the same design from the diagram that you drew. The problems come when there's no shared convention — when a line between two boxes means "connected somehow" with nothing to distinguish ownership from reference from inheritance.

Knowing the standard conventions, even loosely, prevents that ambiguity.

## What the AI Era Actually Changes

I want to be honest about this because there's a lot of noise on both sides.

**AI does not change what fundamentals are.** Hash maps are still hash maps. TCP still has a three-way handshake. Indexes still have trade-offs. These are not going away.

**AI changes how quickly bad decisions become production code.** Before AI tools, writing code took time, and that time created natural friction to think. A senior engineer writing a query by hand might stop and think "wait, this column needs an index." AI generates 50 lines in 3 seconds. The human reviews it in 5 seconds. The index is missing. It ships.

**AI generates plausible code, not correct code.** There is a consistent pattern: AI produces code that works for the happy path, in a test environment, at small scale. The edge cases, the failure modes, the performance at 1M rows — those require understanding to catch.

<MermaidDiagram

The engineers who are thriving with AI tools right now are the ones who use AI to generate and use fundamentals to evaluate. The engineers who are struggling are the ones who generate and ship without reviewing.

### Using AI to learn fundamentals

This side of the equation gets undersold. Most discussion about AI in engineering focuses on AI as a code generator. But AI is also a useful learning tool for fundamentals — if you approach it as active practice rather than answer-seeking.

Some approaches that I've found work well:

**Generate practice problems on demand.** Instead of working through textbook exercises: "Give me 5 real-world scenarios where using a hash map would be the wrong choice, and explain the trade-off in each." You can keep asking follow-up questions, which textbooks don't allow.

**Work through EXPLAIN outputs with help.** Take a query from your actual codebase, run EXPLAIN ANALYZE, and paste the output. Ask AI to walk through what each step means. Then verify the key claims against your database's documentation. AI explanation combined with your own verification builds understanding faster than docs alone.

**Deliberate error spotting.** Ask AI to write code with a specific class of flaw — "write a query with an N+1 problem" or "write a concurrent counter with a race condition" — and then find it yourself. You control the difficulty, and you can ask for a hint or explanation afterward.

**Probe a concept with follow-up questions.** "Explain why TCP connection establishment affects database query latency" — then follow up: "what does that mean for the default timeout values in a JDBC connection pool?" You can follow your actual confusion instead of a textbook's assumed learning path.

The distinction matters: using AI to skip understanding vs. using AI to accelerate understanding. Both result in content being explained to you. Only one results in you actually knowing it when you need it at 2am during an incident.

## Harsh Truth — On Layoffs and Who Survives Them

Nobody writes this in the "learn your fundamentals" posts, so I will.

Every company has cash flow. When headcount decisions happen, the question being answered — whether out loud or not — is: what is the output-to-cost ratio for each person? If one engineer with solid fundamentals and good AI tooling can produce what two engineers without those produced before, the math is not complicated. Headcount decisions are cash flow decisions.

It's not personal. It's not about loyalty. Companies are not families, regardless of how many all-hands presentations describe them that way.

The engineers who survive repeated cuts, or who land somewhere better immediately after getting caught in one, tend to be the ones who can evaluate code, debug production issues, and make architectural decisions — whether they wrote the first draft themselves or reviewed what an agent produced. That person could be anywhere. They don't have to be at the company where the layoffs happen. Companies that just did a round of cuts are also companies that are now hiring selectively — often for exactly that profile.

Strong fundamentals don't make you immune. A bad product line or a bad quarter takes down good engineers too. But they change what happens *after* — how fast you land, and whether you go sideways or upward.

Now, if you truly want zero layoff risk, there is exactly one option: open your own business. Nobody can fire you. No performance review touches you as a founder.

But heads up — it comes with its own set of problems. Revenue that shows up late, or doesn't. Customers who ghost you after three calls. Servers that go down at 3am and the on-call is just... you. No colleague to rubber duck with at 11pm. No paid leave. The slow quiet question around month eight of whether this was the right call.

No layoffs. Different problems entirely. Pick your risk profile.

## A Realistic Study Path for 2026

This is not a "read these 10 books" list. This is what I would prioritize if I had one hour a day, depending on where I am.

**If you're preparing for your first role (pre-employment):**

Focus on reaching the floor, not the ceiling. Build something — a basic API with a database behind it. Make it do CRUD. Understand what query runs when you insert a record. Know what HTTP status code your endpoint returns on each scenario and why.

You don't need EXPLAIN, isolation levels, or race conditions before day one. You need to be able to read code and have some intuition for what it's doing. Get that first.

**If you're in your first two years:**

Start deepening databases. Understand indexes, learn to read EXPLAIN output, recognize N+1 when you see it. This has the highest return on daily backend work.

Then networking. Go deeper than just knowing HTTP exists — understand what happens at each layer when a request fails, and which status code tells you where to look.

Then data structures and complexity. Not LeetCode. Find a resource that teaches the *why* behind structures, not just the implementation.

Also start learning to sketch class diagrams. Pick a module in your codebase and draw the relationships between its main classes. You don't need a tool — paper or a whiteboard is fine. The point is building the habit of thinking in relationships, not just in code.

**If you're moving toward mid-level (2–5 years):**

Operating systems and concurrency. This is the gap I see most often at this level. Understand threading models, memory management, and file descriptors.

System design — the failure mode kind. For every external dependency in your current system, trace through the failure modes. This is practice you can do at work, with real systems.

**What to use:**

- *Designing Data-Intensive Applications* by Martin Kleppmann — for databases and distributed systems, there is no better book
- *Computer Networking: A Top-Down Approach* — for networking fundamentals
- *The Linux Command Line* — for OS fluency
- Your own production code — read your EXPLAIN outputs, trace your request flows, understand your own stack

**One concrete thing to do this week:**

If you have a codebase: take any query that runs on a table with more than 10,000 rows, run EXPLAIN on it, and see if it's doing a sequential scan.

If you don't have a codebase yet: build one. Set up a PostgreSQL database locally, create a table with a few columns, insert some rows, and write a SELECT with a WHERE clause. Then run EXPLAIN on it. See what it says. That one exercise is worth more than an hour of reading about indexes abstractly.

## Closing

Fundamentals are not the gatekeeping trivia that interview culture made them feel like. They are what lets you think clearly about code — whether you're debugging a production incident, reviewing a colleague's PR, or evaluating what an AI generated in three seconds.

The engineers who will grow fastest in the next few years are not the ones who prompt best. They're the ones who prompt well and *evaluate correctly*. And evaluation is built on understanding what you're looking at.

You don't need to know everything. You need to know enough to ask the right questions about the code in front of you.

Start with databases. Be honest about what you don't know. Build understanding, not just familiarity. The rest follows.
