## Introduction

This is the third post in the backend system design series — same format as always: start with the most naive thing that works, then let real-world constraints push it into shape.

Last time I built a [push notification system](/blog/design-push-notification-system/) — one channel, taken from a direct FCM call all the way to a high-volume production pipeline. This post zooms out a level: the **omnichannel delivery backbone** — the thing that, when a send is triggered, takes a prepared audience and drives it out across **push, email, and SMS**, and writes into the in-app **inbox**. The push system from last post becomes just one of the channels this backbone calls.

Boiled right down, the job is simple to state: **you've got a list — usually a big one — of target users, and you want to deliver something to them through a particular method.** Everything below is what that one sentence turns into once the list is huge and the "method" is more than a single channel.

One thing worth pinning down, because it shapes the whole design: the push system from last post is a **general service any upstream can call directly**. When a payment service fires a "payment received" push, it calls push straight — not through any of this (and in practice only some upstream services ever needed push at all). **This backbone is the other path** — it's what drives **promotional / campaign** sends and **timeline (inbox) notifications**: the scheduled, whole-audience, multi-channel stuff. Transactional goes direct; promotional and timeline come through here.

![Two paths: transactional push goes direct to the push system; promotional and timeline go through the delivery backbone](/assets/images/blogs/design-omnichannel-delivery/transactional-vs-promotional.png)

To make it concrete: the way I actually ran this was for **marketing's promotional campaigns**. Marketing defines a campaign, an upstream system prepares and chunks the target audience, the send gets scheduled, and the backbone fans each chunk out across push and the in-app inbox — with SMS kept as a paid fallback. That's the running example I'll lean on for the rest of the post. But notice the machinery underneath doesn't actually care that it's a "campaign" — it only ever sees an audience, a message, and a method.

One scoping note up front: *building* the audience — segmenting a huge user base, working out who's eligible — is its own system (a later post). Here I'll assume the audience already arrives **prepared and chunked**, and focus on what the backbone does once a schedule hits.

The road, each step forced by the one before it:

1. A **list + a worker** that reads and sends
2. **Fire on a schedule**, wake per chunk
3. **Consume a prepared, chunked audience** instead of querying it yourself
4. **Reach more than one channel** — push, email, SMS (and the inbox service)
5. A **delivery workflow** (chunks → workers, or parent/child workflows)
6. **Frequency caps and cost-aware routing**

## The naive version — a list and a worker

You have to start somewhere, and in the real world the honest start is dead simple: you get a **list of targets** from somewhere, and send to each one. "Somewhere" is flexible:

- a **CSV someone uploads** (multipart),
- a **REST call** with the targets right in the JSON body, or
- a **query against a DB**.

The transport genuinely doesn't matter yet; conceptually it's all the same thing — *a source of targets, in some way* — and a worker reads them one by one and sends. Each entry carries what a send needs: a `userId`, the message (or a `templateId`), maybe the channel, plus a bit of metadata.

```
on run:                              # targets from somewhere — upload, REST body, or DB
  for t in load_targets():           # each: userId, message/templateId, metadata
      send(t.userId, t.message)      # one channel, inline
```

<MermaidDiagram chart={`
flowchart LR
    UP["Multipart upload\n(CSV / file)"] --> SRC["Target source"]
    API["REST call\n(targets in JSON body)"] --> SRC
    DB[("DB query\n(stored targets)")] --> SRC
    SRC --> WK["Worker"]
    WK --> LOOP["for each target"]
    LOOP --> SEND["send() — one channel"]
`} />

No scheduler, no queue, no pipeline — you point it at a source and run it. And it only ever needs three things, which every target entry carries:

- an **audience** (the list),
- a **message** (per entry),
- a **method** (the channel).

For a few thousand targets on one channel this genuinely works, and plenty of real sends never grow past it.

Worth saying out loud, because it's easy to forget under all the architecture talk: this dead-simple version is *legitimate engineering*. Even senior engineers ship a lot of "just read the file and send" — the minimum that hits the goal beats the clever thing nobody needed. We only add machinery below when a concrete problem forces it.

The thing to notice is what it quietly assumes: it's **send-as-you-go** — someone uploads the CSV from the back-office and it fires right then, to one channel. And picture who's doing that upload: the **marketing team**. The campaign has to go out Saturday morning, so a real person has to stop whatever their weekend is — family, a date, just rest — to log in and babysit a send. Remote or in-office, this was never about *where* they sit; it's their **time**, the one thing none of us gets back. They'll do it out of duty, and you respect that — but it's exactly the kind of chore we should take off their plate. What they actually want is to set the campaign up *ahead of time* and have it fire on its own. That's the first real constraint — timing — and in my case it's what pushed us past "run it now" to a scheduler (and eventually the whole backbone).

## Fire on a schedule, wake per chunk

**The problem:** we just hit the first one — **timing**. "Fire when the job runs" can't express "fire at 10am Saturday," and the marketing team shouldn't have to spend their weekend being that trigger. On top of that there's **scale**: one process that loads 60M recipients and loops holds the whole audience in memory while a single worker owns the entire send — the flooding problem all over again.

**The fix:** split *when* from *doing*. A **scheduler** owns the schedule — a durable entry that fires at the right time — and when it triggers, it doesn't send anything itself. It hands the send off to be processed in **chunks**, where each chunk is a slice of the audience a worker can pick up on its own.

    CHUNKS --> WK["Delivery workers\n(one chunk at a time)"]
`} />

Two things this gets us. The scheduler is **durable** — a crash doesn't lose the scheduled fire, because it's a persisted entry, not an in-memory timer (your Saturday 10am send still goes out even if the box that scheduled it is long gone). And the work is **horizontally drainable** — N chunks across M workers, with no single process owning 60M rows.

The scheduler itself has a ladder of its own, too. At its simplest it's a **single cron** — one entry that fires one batch at the set time, which is plenty for a single recurring send. But once you've got many sends from different sources, each on its own clock (Saturday 10am here, weekday 9am there, an ad-hoc blast in between), one cron line stops cutting it — you want a **distributed / managed scheduler** that holds many schedule entries and fires each at its own time across workers (clustered Quartz, a scheduling service, or a workflow engine's cron). Same idea, just many schedules instead of one.

But where do those chunks actually come from? That's the next question — and the answer is deliberately *not* "query them right here."

## Consume a prepared audience — don't query it here

**The problem:** the obvious move is to let the delivery job run the audience query itself at fire time — `SELECT ... WHERE eligible`. At 60M that's a monster query competing with everything else, it's slow, and it welds delivery to the segmentation logic. Worst of all you're running it under time pressure — the send is firing *now* — which is the worst possible moment to hammer the database.

**The fix:** separate *who* from *deliver to them*. An upstream system prepares the audience ahead of time — segments it, materializes the recipient list, and writes it out as **chunks** (a chunk being a bounded slice of recipients, sized so one worker can chew through it). When the schedule fires, the delivery side queries nothing — it just **reads chunk by chunk and sends**.

<MermaidDiagram chart={`
flowchart LR
    PREP["Audience prep\n(upstream, ahead of time)"] --> STORE[("Prepared audience\n(chunks)")]
    SCHED["Scheduler fires"] --> WK["Delivery workers"]
    STORE -->|"read chunk"| WK
    WK --> SEND["Send"]
`} />

This is exactly what I ran: the audience was prepared and chunked well ahead of time, and the delivery side's whole job was to read its chunk and push it out. Two wins fall out of it — the heavy segmentation work happens off the critical path (not while the send is firing), and delivery scales by simply adding workers that each grab the next unread chunk.

How the audience actually gets built and chunked — the segmentation, the ETL behind it — is a system of its own, and a post of its own later in this series. Here it's enough that the chunks show up ready to read.

And "prepared" can be as humble as it sounds — remember the naive version just pulled targets from some source (an upload, a REST body, a DB). At scale that barely changes: the audience can still be a **CSV marketing drops in**, only now it's split into chunks and read the same way. You reach for the heavy Audience Platform (real segmentation, ETL) only when the targeting genuinely needs it — a list upload covers a surprising amount.

So far, though, everything's still gone out on one channel. "Reach the customer" almost never means only push.

## Reach more than one channel — omnichannel dispatch

**The problem:** push alone misses people — notifications are switched off, the app's been uninstalled, or the message matters enough that you want email or SMS as backup. "Deliver to the customer" really means meeting them on whatever channel actually reaches them.

**The fix:** the backbone reads a chunk and, per recipient, **dispatches to one or more channels**. Each channel sits behind a common interface (the same Strategy idea as the push handler from last post) — but the part that bites is that **the channels aren't symmetric**. A quick tour of what makes each one its own animal:

- **Push** — the system from the last post; the backbone just calls it. Fast and cheap, but ephemeral.
- **Email** — asynchronous and slow; you hand it to a provider (SES, SendGrid) and live with bounces, spam reputation, and the gap between "accepted" and "actually in the inbox."
- **SMS** — the expensive one. You pay per message, carriers rate-limit you, and you're boxed in by a hard length limit — so SMS is usually a deliberate *fallback*, not a default.
- **Inbox** — and this one isn't a "send" at all (more on that next).

And to be clear on scope: **email and SMS are external providers** — SES/SendGrid for email, an SMS gateway for SMS — that you integrate behind the adapter. You're not building a mail server or becoming a carrier; the backbone's whole value is the orchestration *around* these channels, not the channels themselves.

**The inbox is a neighbour service, not a channel you "send" to.** There's no provider, no network delivery — "delivering" to the inbox is literally **inserting a row into a DB**, a per-user feed (the bell-icon timeline) the user pulls up whenever they feel like it. Because nothing is pushed, it's the channel for the **non-urgent** stuff — it sits there until they look. Items are grouped (promotion / system / customer support), each an image-optional card with a message that deeplinks into the app or out to the web. And it owns a whole **read side** the other channels don't have: pagination, read/unread state, the unread badge. The backbone just writes the record; everything after is the inbox service's job. (Often you do both — fire a push *and* write the inbox row — so tapping the push lands on the stored item.)

## The delivery workflow — the part you can't skip

**The problem:** dispatching a whole chunk reliably is not a fire-and-forget loop. Every recipient has to be sent, tracked, and retried on a transient failure; a worker crash mid-chunk mustn't double-send or lose its place; and you need to know what actually happened to each one. That's a **workflow** — and unlike the fallback niceties below, it isn't optional. It's the core of this stage.

**The fix:** make the chunk's processing a real workflow. Two shapes work, and they're the two ends of the ladder you've already seen:

- **One worker, fanned out over Kafka.** A worker reads a chunk and splits it into per-recipient (or sub-batch) messages on Kafka; stateless delivery workers consume them, send, retry, and dead-letter. It scales by partitions and consumers, and the "state" lives in Kafka offsets plus your result store.
- **A parent workflow that spawns child workflows.** A parent workflow per send fans out **N child workflows** — one per chunk (and, if you want, a grandchild per recipient). Each child owns its own state, retries, and timers. This is the Cadence/Temporal parent-child pattern.

Either way the must-have is identical: every recipient in the chunk ends in a *known* state — sent, failed, or given up — and a crash resumes instead of replaying blindly.

<MermaidDiagram chart={`
flowchart TD
    PARENT["Parent workflow\n(per send)"] --> C1["Child workflow\nchunk 1"]
    PARENT --> C2["Child workflow\nchunk 2"]
    PARENT --> CN["Child workflow\nchunk N"]
    C1 --> S1["send + track + retry"]
    C2 --> S2["send + track + retry"]
    CN --> SN["send + track + retry"]
`} />

**Fallback is a nice-to-have on top.** Once the workflow exists, adding a fallback chain is cheap: try push, and if it doesn't land within a window, fall back to SMS or email. Worth it for messages that genuinely matter — but it's an enhancement, not the reason the workflow exists.

How you *build* this workflow follows the same rule as everything else in the series — start from the constraint, not the tool — so climb this ladder only as far as something actually forces you:

- **Just send + retry, nothing to wait on?** Plain **cron + queue + stateless workers**, the same shape as the push pipeline. Hand-roll the retry; there's nothing to orchestrate yet.
- **Chunk processing that has to restart cleanly after a crash?** **Spring Batch** — read → process → write in chunks, restartable, partitionable. Built for exactly "read the chunk and send."
- **Mostly scheduling + retried background jobs?** **JobRunr** or **Quartz** give durable scheduling and retries (with a dashboard) without standing up a workflow cluster.
- **A genuinely stateful per-chunk / per-recipient flow** — a parent fanning out child workflows, with durable state, retries, and timers (and timed fallback if you add it)? *Now* a workflow engine — **Cadence/Temporal**, or managed **Step Functions** — earns its weight, because that's the whole reason it exists.

I landed on **Cadence** (Uber's durable workflow engine; Temporal is the fork of it) because that last case was mine: a parent workflow fanning out child workflows per chunk, each owning its own state, retries, and timers — letting the engine own all that beat hand-rolling it, and its durable timers cover the scheduled fire too (the weekend send is just a workflow that starts on a timer). But if your delivery is only ever send-and-retry, reaching for an orchestrator is precisely the over-engineering this series keeps warning about. The machinery has to be pulled in by the problem, not the other way around.

And if you *do* add that fallback, the honest caveat from the push post applies: a push "delivered" only means the provider accepted it, so "fall back if not delivered" leans on the **client-side delivery signal** from last time. Without it, fallback really means "fall back only if the provider outright rejected it" — weaker. Pick your trigger knowing which signal you actually have.

## Frequency caps and cost — what to weigh

Once one person is reachable on several channels, the backbone is the only place that sees them all at once — so two decisions land here.

**Frequency cap — two layers.** Cap the **total** touches per person across all channels (the cap belongs to the *person*, not the pipe), *and* cap per channel. The two interact: say a person tolerates **4/day total**, but the company chooses **1 push + 1 email** and leaves the other 2 unused. That's deliberate — you're balancing business reach against annoying the user, and unused headroom is fine. Erring toward fewer beats getting muted.

When sends compete for a slot under the cap, you need a **priority** — from a message **attribute** (a priority field) or an **order marketing ranks in the UI**. *Where* that priority lives overlaps with the Audience Platform; my take is to keep hard **cap enforcement** at the backbone (only it sees all channels live) and let upstream decide *who's eligible and in what order*. The line is genuinely blurry.

**Cost-aware routing.** Prefer the cheap, high-reach channels (push, inbox); treat SMS as a fallback you *chose* to pay for. Every push that lands is an SMS you never sent — leaning off costly SMS this way turns into real, recurring budget saved, not a rounding error.

## If you want to build it yourself — tech by pattern

You don't have to write any of this from scratch; mature tools cover each piece. Pick by the pattern you actually need — and remember the ladder from earlier: only climb as far as the problem forces you.

**Scheduling the fire — [Quartz](https://www.quartz-scheduler.org/)** (JVM), or a cloud scheduler (AWS EventBridge Scheduler, Kubernetes CronJob).

- Define a job + a cron/trigger, and persist it to a DB-backed store so the fire survives a restart.
- On fire, don't do the work — enqueue the send for workers to drain.
- Feasible when: you just need durable timing. The lightest option.

**Reading & processing chunks — [Spring Batch](https://spring.io/projects/spring-batch)** (JVM).

- `ItemReader` reads a chunk → `ItemProcessor` builds the per-recipient payload → `ItemWriter` dispatches it.
- Commit per chunk, so a crash restarts from the last good chunk, not the top.
- Partition the step to run chunks in parallel across workers.
- Feasible when: bounded, chunk-shaped work with no long per-item waits. A great default for JVM shops.

**Stateful per-chunk / per-recipient workflow — [Temporal](https://temporal.io/) / [Cadence](https://cadenceworkflow.io/)** (self-hosted), or [AWS Step Functions](https://aws.amazon.com/step-functions/) (managed).

- A **parent workflow** fans out **child workflows** per chunk; each send is an activity (`sendPush`, `sendSMS`, …).
- The engine owns retries and state, and durable timers cover both the scheduled fire and any fallback window.
- A crashed worker resumes mid-flow — no double-send, no lost place.
- Feasible when: the per-chunk flow is genuinely stateful (parent/child, timers, retries). Heaviest, but it owns the hard parts. (This is what I used — Cadence.)

**Lighter background jobs + retries — [JobRunr](https://www.jobrunr.io/)** (JVM).

- Enqueue jobs and get automatic retries plus a dashboard, all backed by your DB.
- Feasible when: you want scheduling + retried jobs without standing up a workflow cluster, and the fallback logic is simple.

**Preparing & chunking the audience — [Apache Spark](https://spark.apache.org/)** (or Flink).

- A batch job reads the source data, applies the segmentation rules, and writes the recipient list out as chunks — all off the critical path, ahead of the fire.
- Feasible when: the audience is huge and the segmentation is heavy. (This is really the upstream Audience Platform — a post of its own — and the delivery backbone just consumes its chunks.)

None of these is "the answer" on its own — each maps to one pattern. Match the tool to the constraint, not the hype.

## Get back to your goal — you just want to talk to your users

After all the schedulers, chunks, workflows, and caps, it's worth stepping back to the one thing this whole post is in service of: **you just want to communicate with your users.** That's it. Everything above is plumbing in service of that sentence.

So let the goal pick the plumbing, not the other way around:

- A one-off message to a handful of people? A list and a `send()` — you're done.
- Need it to go out while the team's off on the weekend? Add the scheduler.
- Reaching people who've muted push? Add a second channel.
- A 60M blast that can't melt the system or double-send? *Now* the chunks and the workflow earn their place.

If a piece of this architecture isn't making it easier or cheaper to reach your users — or protecting them from being spammed — it doesn't belong in *your* version yet. The backbone is worth building when the goal genuinely outgrows the simple loop. Until then, the simple loop **is** the right design.

## Wrapping up

We started with a file and a worker that loops a list and sends on one channel, and ended with a backbone that fires on a schedule, reads a prepared audience chunk by chunk, and drives each recipient through an omnichannel workflow. The path, in order:

1. **Split scheduling from sending** so a send fires reliably and the work is drainable in chunks.
2. **Consume a prepared, chunked audience** instead of querying 60M rows while the send is firing.
3. **Dispatch across channels** — push, email, SMS — behind one interface, respecting that they're not symmetric.
4. **Treat the inbox as a neighbour service** you write to, not a channel you fire.
5. **Make delivery a real workflow** — fan a chunk to workers (via Kafka) or to child workflows; fallback is a bonus on top.
6. **Weigh frequency caps and cost at the backbone** — the one place that sees every channel — including priority when sends compete under the cap.

And it's worth grounding why this is worth building at all. The real version — a timeline-notification delivery system I designed and built (Kotlin / Spring Boot WebFlux + Cadence Workflow + Kafka) — was about transforming a **back-office manual upload** into a **scheduled** one. Since this delivery layer was meant to be the primary backbone for our communication channels, I designed it so the operator and marketing teams could set things up with far less effort — and spend their time on the things that actually matter to them, instead of babysitting a send. There were impact numbers too, but those came as a side effect. The value I really care about belongs to the people: their time, handed back.

The meta-point holds from the last two posts: **nobody needs this on day one.** Most apps are a single `send()` on one channel, reading a list someone handed you — and that's completely fine. This backbone only earns its keep once you're running *scheduled, multi-channel sends to a large, prepared audience*. Don't build the conductor before you have an orchestra to conduct.
