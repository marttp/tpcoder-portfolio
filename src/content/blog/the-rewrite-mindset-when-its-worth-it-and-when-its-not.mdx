Every engineer has felt it. You open a legacy codebase, scroll through a few files, and the thought appears almost immediately: **"We should just rewrite this."**

I have been there more times than I can count. After years of working across payment systems, growth platforms, and enterprise banking — touching codebases that range from clean to almost ancient — I have developed a more careful view on the rewrite urge. It is not always wrong. But it is almost always more expensive than you think, and the value delivered to customers is often exactly the same.

Here is what I have learned about when rewrites make sense, when they do not, and how to think through the decision honestly.

---

## The Rewrite Urge — Where It Comes From

Before talking about whether to rewrite, it helps to understand _why_ the urge is so strong. In my experience, it usually comes from one of these triggers:

1. **Frustration with technical debt.** The code works, but every change feels like defusing a bomb. You spend more time understanding workarounds than building features.
2. **New technology excitement.** A new framework or language solves problems cleanly that the current stack handles awkwardly. The gap between "what we have" and "what we could have" feels hard to ignore.
3. **Team turnover.** New engineers join, cannot understand the existing code, and decide it is broken at its core rather than just unfamiliar.
4. **Ego and ownership.** Let me be honest — sometimes the urge to rewrite is about wanting to build something that feels like _yours_. The existing code was written by someone else, and rebuilding it feels more satisfying than maintaining it.

None of these are invalid feelings. But feelings are not a business case.

---

## The Hidden Equation: Cost In, Same Value Out

Here is the part that took me years to fully accept: **from the customer's perspective, a rewrite delivers the same value.** The user does not care whether your backend runs on a legacy Akka-based multi-tenant system or a clean Spring Boot 3.2 application with Kotlin coroutines. They care whether the feature works, whether it is fast enough, and whether it is reliable.

This diagram is the thing I wish someone had drawn for me earlier in my career. The green boxes — customer value — are identical. The red boxes — what the company absorbs — are big. A rewrite is basically spending engineering budget to arrive at the same customer outcome through a different path.

But here is the part I want to be honest about: **same customer value does not mean a rewrite is pointless.** The value you gain is often not on the customer side — it is on the engineering side. Lower maintenance cost, better developer experience, fewer hours spent working around problems that should not exist. These are real costs the company pays every sprint, and sometimes a rewrite is the only way to stop paying them.

The question is not "does the customer get more value?" The question is: **"Is the maintenance and DX cost high enough to justify the rewrite investment?"** Without that, there is no strong case. With it, the case builds itself.

## The Decision Framework

Over time, I have built a mental framework for checking rewrite decisions. It is not perfect, but it has saved me from a few bad calls and confirmed a few good ones.

A few things to notice about this flow:

- **It starts with the business, not the code.** The first question is not "is the code bad?" — it is "is the system blocking business goals?" Bad code that ships features and makes money is more valuable than beautiful code that does not exist yet.
- **Data comes before proposals.** If you cannot measure the problem — incident count, maintenance hours, cost of workarounds — you are not ready to propose a rewrite. You are just frustrated.
- **Step-by-step fixes are the default.** The framework points toward gradual improvement in most cases. A full rewrite is the exception, not the rule.

## When Rewriting Made Sense — A Real Example

I want to share a case where a rewrite was the right call, because I think the reasoning matters more than the outcome.

At PayPay, I rebuilt the personalized asset delivery system. The legacy system was a multi-tenant Akka-based solution inherited from a previous Paytm implementation. Here is what the situation actually looked like:

- **80% of the codebase was dead code.** Not "probably unused" — confirmed dead. Paytm-specific logic that had no relevance to PayPay's use case but was still deployed and maintained.
- **Nobody wanted to touch it.** The codebase was so massive and tangled that engineers avoided making changes. When no one wants to maintain a system, that system is already dying — you just have not noticed yet.
- **Akka changed its license.** Akka moved from open source to the Business Source License. This was an outside pressure that made staying on the current stack a growing risk — not just technically, but legally and financially.
- **The tech stack did not match the team's ecosystem.** PayPay uses different languages depending on the workload — you pick what fits. And Akka did not fit. It was a framework nobody else in the organization used for this kind of workload, which meant every onboarding, every code review, every incident required context that only existed in this one service.
- **Could not align with company standard.** PayPay has a golden image — a standardized base infrastructure that every service should use. The legacy Akka system could not align with it, which meant deploying, monitoring, and operating it was different from every other service in the organization.

The rewrite replaced it with a clean Spring Boot 3.2 Kotlin application using coroutines and async caching. Performance improved around 10-20% — not a dramatic jump, but that was never the point. The real wins were elsewhere: 80% dead code eliminated, the team could now maintain the system using a stack that fits PayPay's ecosystem, and monthly infrastructure savings of 648,720 yen.

**Why this rewrite was justified:**

1. The codebase was unmaintainable — not in theory, but in practice (no one would touch it)
2. An external license change created real risk for staying on Akka
3. The tech stack was isolated from the rest of the organization
4. The maintenance and DX cost was measurable (hours lost navigating dead code, onboarding friction, infrastructure waste)
5. Fixing it step by step was not realistic (the dead code was deeply tangled with live code)

Notice how "better performance" is not the main reason. The performance gain was modest. What made the rewrite worth it was the maintenance burden, the license risk, and the developer experience cost that the team was paying every single sprint.

## When Rewriting Did Not Make Sense — The Step-by-Step Path

In contrast, when I managed the Spring Boot upgrade at PayPay's Push Notification ecosystem — from version 2.2 with JDK 8 to 3.3 with JDK 17 — it might seem like rewriting from scratch on the new stack would be better. Clean start, modern patterns, no legacy baggage. Right?

But it would have been the wrong call. The existing services were stable, serving production traffic, and the team understood them well. What they needed was an upgrade, not a replacement.

Rewriting this entire ecosystem at once would take too long and carry too much risk. To keep the system running without interruption and maintain ownership throughout, I planned a multi-stage upgrade over 3 months:

- JDK 8 to 17 first
- Spring Boot 2.2 to 3.x after the JDK migration stabilized
- Build system migration from Maven to Gradle in a separate phase
- Integration with PayPay's golden image as the final step

The service was never offline. There were no regression surprises. The team maintained full context throughout. And the end result was the same modern stack we would have gotten from a rewrite — without the risk or the lost team knowledge. Though I will be honest — there were 2 brief downtime incidents during the process. Not perfect, but far better than a full rewrite would have been.

## Another Simple Prevention — Deployment Strategy

Whether you are doing a full rewrite or an incremental upgrade, the thing that reduces release risk the most is picking the right deployment strategy. Not just deploying and hoping for the best.

**Blue-Green Deployment** — run the old and new environments in parallel. Deploy the new version to the green environment, then switch traffic over from blue. If something breaks, switch back immediately. No slow rollback, no downtime. During the Spring Boot upgrade across the Push Notification ecosystem, having this kind of strategy is what kept the damage to just 2 brief incidents instead of something much worse.

**Feature Flag Release** — deploy new code to production but hide it behind a flag. Turn it on for internal users first. Then gradually roll out to real users — 1%, 10%, 50%. If something goes wrong, kill the flag. No deployment rollback needed. This works especially well for rewrites that change behavior, because you can test against real production traffic without impacting all users at once.

Neither of these eliminates the risk of a rewrite. But they give you a kill switch — and having a kill switch makes it much easier to ship with confidence, because if you get it wrong, you can get back fast.

## The Smaller Rewrite — Replacing a Technology or Framework

Not every rewrite is a full system rebuild. Sometimes it is swapping a build tool, replacing a library, or migrating an API integration. These feel smaller — and that is exactly what makes them dangerous. The expected cost looks low ("it is just a library swap"), so engineers skip the same careful thinking they would apply to a full rewrite.

But the hidden costs add up quietly: migration bugs that only show up in edge cases, team members who now need to relearn tooling, config drift between old and new patterns during the transition, and documentation that becomes half-outdated overnight.

For example — at PayPay, I noticed the FCM legacy API was heading toward deprecation and took action to migrate it to Firebase Admin SDK before it became a problem. If we had not migrated in time, push notifications would have stopped working entirely. Nobody asked me to do it. I caught it, raised it, and drove the migration myself. But I have also seen the other side. Engineers proposing to replace a working ORM because a newer one has a cleaner API. Teams wanting to swap testing frameworks mid-project because the new one "feels better." Build tool migrations justified as "modern best practice."

The same decision framework applies at every scale. Before swapping a technology, ask:

- **Is the current tool blocking something concrete?** Not "it is annoying" — actually blocking.
- **Is there outside pressure to change?** Deprecation, security vulnerability, vendor end-of-life.
- **What is the migration surface area?** A library used in 3 files is different from one used in 300.
- **Will the team need to relearn?** If so, factor that cost in honestly. It is never zero.

The smaller the change looks on paper, the more likely it is that the hidden costs get ignored. That is where the scars come from.

And side projects do not have to be work-related — sometimes you are just curious about how something works and want to try building it. That is enough.

## The 2026 Reality — New Tech, AI Rewrites, and What "Modern" Even Means

There is something worth stepping back and thinking about: **if there was no legacy, there would be no "modern."** Every new framework, every new language, every new tool you see out there exists because someone looked at the old way and said "this hurts, let me fix it." Legacy is the teacher. Without it, we would not know what problems to solve.

So when you look at a legacy codebase and feel the urge to rewrite — remember that the new tool you want to use was born from exactly that kind of pain. And one day, the thing you build today will be someone else's legacy. That is not a failure. That is how software works.

### Most New Tech Solves Engineering Pain — Not Business Pain

Look at the programming languages and frameworks released in recent years. Most of them solve **engineering pain points**: better developer experience, faster builds, cleaner syntax, easier concurrency, simpler deployment. These are real improvements. But very few of them solve **business pain points** or **security pain points** directly.

If your current system is slow to develop on but it serves customers well and meets security requirements — a new framework does not change the business outcome. It changes how the team feels while working. That matters, but it is a different kind of value, and it should be weighed differently when you are making the case for a rewrite.

### AI Can Rewrite Faster — But Who Takes the Penalty?

In the world of AI coding tools, yes — you can rewrite a whole system with more speed and more confidence than ever before. AI can generate code fast, cover edge cases you might miss, and produce something that looks clean and modern in a fraction of the time.

But here is the question nobody asks enough: **do you trust AI enough to rewrite your production system if you are the one who takes the penalty when it breaks?**

Because that is the reality. AI generates the code. You own the result. When something goes wrong in production at 3 AM, the incident report does not say "AI wrote this." It says your name. And what happens after the AI-powered rewrite? You end up reviewing AI-generated code for technical debt, finding patterns you did not ask for, fixing assumptions the AI made that do not match your system. You get stuck in a cycle: rewrite with AI, review, fix, review again.

And reviewing code you did not write, for a system you are now responsible for, is its own kind of burden. Before using AI to justify a rewrite, ask yourself: am I ready to own every line it produces?

## For Yourself, For Your Team, For the Company

Rewrite decisions look different depending on whose perspective you take:

| Perspective         | What "Rewrite" Means                                                       | The Risk                                                 |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **For yourself**    | A chance to build something clean, learn new tech, and feel ownership      | You focus on personal satisfaction, not business value   |
| **For your team**   | A shared investment in a better development experience                     | The team loses speed for months during the transition    |
| **For the company** | A bet that the new system will lower long-term cost or open new capability | The company pays real money for uncertain future returns |

The best rewrite decisions happen when all three perspectives align:

- The engineer gets to work on meaningful architecture
- The team gets a more maintainable system
- The company gets measurable cost reduction or new business capability

When only one perspective benefits — especially if it is just the engineer's — the rewrite is probably not the right call.

## What I Want the Next Generation of Engineers to Know

I have mentored enough junior engineers and new joiners to know that the rewrite urge hits hardest early in your career. You join a team, open a legacy codebase, and your first instinct is to tear it down and rebuild it "properly." I know because I carried that same instinct for years — and I have the scars to prove when it was wrong.

Here is what those scars taught me:

**That ugly, battle-tested codebase you want to replace? It survived production incidents you have not seen yet.** It handles edge cases that are not written down anywhere except in the code itself. Every weird conditional, every pointless-looking null check — there is a production incident behind it that someone debugged at 2 AM. When you rewrite, you lose those lessons. You will rediscover them the hard way.

**Your proposal will be judged on business terms, not engineering terms.** When you walk into a room and say "we should rewrite this," the engineering manager hears a timeline. The product manager hears features that will not get built. The finance team hears salary cost multiplied by months. If you cannot translate your frustration into numbers, expect the answer to be "not now."

**The engineers you admire most are probably not the ones who rewrite everything.** They are the ones who can work through a messy codebase, find the real problem, fix it precisely, and move on. That discipline is not a lack of ambition — it is a sign of experience. Knowing when to leave code alone is a skill that takes years to build, and it is one of the most valuable skills you can have.

**Learn to tell the difference between "this system needs to change" and "I want to change this system."** They feel the same from the inside. But the first one has evidence — incidents, slow onboarding, rising maintenance hours. The second one has excitement. Both are real, but only one belongs in a proposal.

One more thing: **the cost of a rewrite is not just engineering time. It is team trust.** If you push for a rewrite, deliver it late, and introduce regressions, the next time anyone on your team proposes a rewrite — even a justified one — the organization will remember. Spend that trust wisely.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **The customer value math is harsh.** A rewrite often delivers the same value to users at a much higher cost to the company. Make sure the reason goes beyond engineering preference.
- **Start with the business case, not the code.** If the system is not blocking business goals, the rewrite is probably not urgent — no matter how painful the code is.
- **Step-by-step improvement is almost always the safer bet.** A staged migration keeps team knowledge, avoids big-bang risk, and often reaches the same destination as a full rewrite.
- **Be honest about your motivation.** The gap between "I want to change this" and "this needs to change" is where most bad rewrite decisions live.
- **Align all three perspectives.** The best rewrites benefit the engineer, the team, and the company at the same time. If only one side wins, reconsider.
