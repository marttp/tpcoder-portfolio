I recently listened to Dalton and Michael's discussion on how to generate unique AI startup ideas. As someone who builds backend systems and thinks a lot about what makes a product worth engineering, this one gave me a lot to reflect on — especially the parts about why most founders end up building the exact same thing.

Here's what I took away.

---

## The Real Bottleneck Isn't Building Anymore

With modern tools — AI coding assistants, cloud platforms, open-source frameworks — the barrier to building is practically zero. You can go from idea to working prototype in a weekend. But that's exactly the problem: **the actual bottleneck is generating a genuinely unique idea**, not shipping code.

This hit close to home. As engineers, we tend to focus on the "how" — architecture, stack choices, performance. But if the "what" is just a clone of whatever recently raised a seed round, none of that matters.

---

## The Consensus Trap

Dalton and Michael describe a pattern where founders default to building clones of whatever startup just got funded. The logic is: "VCs invested in this, so it must be a good idea." But investors often fund the team, not the specific idea — and those funded ideas frequently pivot anyway.

I've noticed a version of this in engineering too. We gravitate toward whatever tech is trending on Hacker News or X. "Everyone's building AI wrappers around GPT, so that must be the opportunity." But if the market is already broadly rewarding something, the window for a unique entry is closing.

**The uncomfortable truth:** if you pitch an idea to your network and everyone agrees it's brilliant, you're probably building a consensus product. The best ideas often make people worried for you.

---

## The "Discard Bin" Strategy

This was the most useful framework for me. Don't try to invent something nobody has ever thought of — that's nearly impossible. Instead, look for ideas that others have considered but discarded because they falsely assumed them to be bad.

In engineering terms, this means targeting problems that others avoid because they're messy: deeply technical challenges, legacy system overhauls, domain-specific complexity that requires years of investment. The architectural difficulty itself becomes your moat.

This reframed how I think about side projects. The interesting problems aren't the ones everyone is solving with a weekend hackathon — they're the ones people looked at and walked away from because the payoff timeline was too long.

---

## The 10-Year Cheat Code

A practical way to escape consensus: commit to a problem that takes 10 years to solve.

Most founders artificially constrain themselves to short timelines — "what can I ship in 3 months and show traction?" — and cut out the most valuable, difficult work in the process. But if you're willing to stay with a hard problem for a decade, you're competing against almost nobody.

As a backend engineer, this resonates. The most defensible systems I've seen aren't the ones that shipped fast — they're the ones that solved deep domain problems over years of iteration. Payment infrastructure, observability platforms, database engines — these weren't weekend projects.

---

## Build What You Wish Existed

Building for your own needs has apparently fallen out of fashion, but it remains one of the most reliable ways to build something fundamentally useful.

The overabundance of VC content has conditioned people to only pursue ideas with an obvious "trillion-dollar TAM." This filters out highly successful products that started as niche solutions to specific frustrations. Many of the developer tools we rely on daily started exactly this way — someone was annoyed enough by a problem to build the tool themselves.

My takeaway: re-anchor your product sense to your own daily frictions. If standard solutions are failing you, build the tool that solves your exact problem. Don't prematurely worry about market size.

---

## Being "Weird" Is an Advantage

This section was surprisingly relevant. Dalton and Michael argue that nonconformists thrive in ideation because they operate outside standard consensus. Conversely, people highly calibrated to traditional academic or management frameworks often struggle to generate unique concepts.

But here's the part that I found most practical: **not everyone has to be the eccentric visionary.** If generating non-consensus ideas isn't your natural strength, the strategic move is to partner with someone who does think that way and position yourself as the execution engine — the person who makes those ideas a reality.

As someone who leans more toward the engineering execution side, I found this honest and refreshing. Knowing your strengths and building a team around them is a better strategy than forcing yourself into a role that doesn't fit.

---

## Stop Seeking Authority Approval

The final point that stuck with me: stop trying to get validation from authority figures or investors before committing.

True conviction means you're willing to work on the problem for years, even when the market initially tells you it's a bad idea. If you require immediate validation to keep building, you'll inevitably pivot toward generic, low-value products.

This applies to engineering decisions too. The best technical bets I've seen weren't the ones that had consensus support from day one — they were the ones where someone had deep conviction based on first principles and was willing to build in the dark for a while.

---

## What I'm Taking Away

If I had to distill this into a checklist for myself:

- Stop using funding announcements or trending repos as a signal for what to build
- Look for problems that others considered but walked away from — the messier, the better
- Build tools that solve my own real frustrations before worrying about TAM
- Lean into execution strength and find collaborators who think differently
- Be willing to commit without external validation

I don't have a unique startup idea right now. But at least now I have a better filter for recognizing when I'm falling into the consensus trap — and that's worth something.
