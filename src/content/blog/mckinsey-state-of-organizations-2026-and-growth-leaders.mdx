I recently went through two McKinsey reports that, when read together, paint a sharp picture of where organizations stand today — and why so few actually win. The first, [*The State of Organizations 2026*](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/the-state-of-organizations), surveyed over 10,000 senior executives across 15 countries on the forces reshaping how companies operate. The second, [*Inspired for Business Growth*](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/growth-marketing-and-sales/our-insights/inspired-for-business-growth-how-five-companies-beat-the-market), analyzed roughly 5,000 companies to find the ~15% that consistently outperformed their peers in profitable revenue growth over the past five years.

The contrast is striking. One report shows an overwhelming majority of leaders feeling unprepared. The other shows a handful of companies that figured out how to grow through the exact same chaos. Reading them side by side forces a question: **what do the winners actually do differently?**

Here's what I took away.

---

## The Three Tectonic Forces Reshaping Everything

McKinsey's *State of Organizations 2026* identifies three structural forces that aren't temporary disruptions — they're permanent shifts:

### 1. Technology Disruption (Especially AI)

88% of organizations are experimenting with AI, but **81% report no meaningful bottom-line gains**. Even more telling: 86% of leaders feel their organizations are not prepared to adopt AI in day-to-day operations, and one in six organizations have no clear C-level owner for AI adoption.

The gap isn't about awareness — it's about execution. Most companies are stuck in "piecemeal adoption," bolting AI onto existing workflows rather than reimagining how work gets done. The report argues that capturing real value requires a **double transformation**: both technological and organizational. This resonates with what I've seen firsthand in engineering organizations — you can give every developer access to Copilot or Claude Code, but if the underlying development process is broken, AI just produces broken code faster.

### 2. Economic and Geopolitical Disruption

Nearly three in four respondents say geopolitical uncertainties have had a notable impact on their organizations. Trade is shifting to closer partners, supply chains are being rethought, and 43% of leaders admit they divested assets too late or failed to do so when they should have.

### 3. Workforce Shifts

Employee expectations, demographics, and tech-driven work models are all changing simultaneously. The old playbooks — structural redesigns, cost cuts, flatter hierarchies — are hitting diminishing returns. Two-thirds of leaders think their organizations are overly complex and inefficient, but traditional remedies aren't fixing it.

Across all three forces, the picture is the same: **72% of leaders say their organizations are not fully ready to face upcoming changes.** Even among optimistic leaders, only one-third feel prepared. The disruption is here, and most organizations are playing catch-up.

---

## Meanwhile, the Top 15% Are Pulling Away

This is where the *Inspired for Business Growth* report gets interesting. Out of ~3,000 companies with $1B+ revenues, only 61 consistently outperformed — beating peers by an average of **five percentage points in revenue growth** and **seven percentage points in profitability** annually.

What separates them isn't luck, timing, or industry tailwinds. It's three deliberate choices:

### Choice 1: Consistent Commitment to Funding Growth

Every CEO says growth matters. But outperformers **actually fund it** — through downturns, through uncertainty, when near-term returns are unclear. They invest multiples more than competitors during tough times.

**Walmart** is the clearest example. While others slowed spending during and after the pandemic, Walmart continued investing in e-commerce, retail media, and marketplace capabilities. More than half of Walmart's operating-income growth now comes from newer platforms that didn't exist a decade ago. Its advertising business alone accounted for roughly 30% of operating profit in 2024.

**JPMorgan Chase** invested $18 billion annually in technology — more than its peers — even through rate volatility and regulatory uncertainty. During COVID, when banks were closing branches, JPMorgan built roughly 900 new ones. When digital adoption accelerated, they pivoted quickly to consolidate. The ability to invest early, learn, and adjust became a defining advantage.

### Choice 2: A Diversified Portfolio of Growth Engines

Outperformers don't rely on a single bet. They strengthen the core, expand into adjacencies, and test new sources of growth simultaneously.

**Builders FirstSource** used M&A not just for scale but to move into higher-margin manufactured components and value-added solutions — reducing exposure to commodity swings. **ASML** expanded beyond lithography systems into software, metrology, and services, deepening customer relationships and creating recurring revenue. **Progressive** built tech-intensive capabilities like telematics (Snapshot for auto, Smart Haul for trucks) that became entirely new growth engines beyond traditional premiums.

The key pattern: these companies **actively manage** their portfolios. They double down on what works and kill what doesn't.

### Choice 3: Technology as an Accelerator, Not a Side Project

This is where the two reports converge most powerfully. While 86% of organizations feel unprepared for AI, the growth outperformers have already made technology central to their operating models.

It's no coincidence that companies like Walmart and Progressive appear here again — their technology investment isn't a separate "innovation lab" story, it's the same growth story viewed from a different angle. Walmart invested in predictive maintenance, AI-enabled customer service across digital and physical channels, and drone delivery — all wired into its core operations. JPMorgan Chase's $18B annual tech spend enabled faster product launches, improved risk management, and enterprise-wide generative AI integration. ASML's customer coinvestment program and supply chain automation gave it a technology moat that peers simply couldn't match.

**The difference isn't that these companies adopted more technology. It's that they integrated technology into how the business actually operates**, rather than treating it as a separate initiative.

## The Organizational Gap: Why Most Companies Can't Execute

Reading both reports together, the pattern becomes clear. The *State of Organizations* report reveals exactly why most companies fail to become the outperformers described in the growth report:

- **They lack end-to-end thinking.** Most AI efforts focus on fragmented use cases. Most productivity efforts focus on functional optimization without changing desired outputs. One firm found it was duplicating 35% of decisions across functions, holding 60% more meetings than peers, and spending 1,000+ hours per month on manual reporting. You can't out-technology that kind of organizational dysfunction.

- **They under-invest in people alongside technology.** 55% of leaders say successfully building AI capabilities of employees will bring exponential productivity gains — but only one in four expect AI agents to act as autonomous teammates in the short term. The winners recognize that AI transformation is as much about people as it is about code.

- **They struggle with resource allocation.** Only 30% of organizations reallocate resources enterprise-wide. Most hoard budgets within functions and protect legacy investments. Meanwhile, growth outperformers dynamically shift capital and talent to wherever the highest-impact opportunities are.

- **Leadership isn't keeping up.** The reports emphasize that leaders need an "inside out" approach — personal growth, self-reflection, and the ability to inspire change. Only 20% of leaders believe nonfinancial rewards can drive employee performance, missing a critical lever. Reflective leaders are nearly twice as likely (30% vs 17%) to believe their organizations can quickly adapt to change.

## What This Means (My Take)

If I step back and think about what these two reports say together, a few things stand out:

- **The AI gap is an execution gap, not a technology gap.** Almost everyone has access to the same tools. The difference is whether organizations rewire their processes and culture around those tools, or just layer them on top of broken workflows. This mirrors what I see in engineering every day — a team using Claude Code or Cursor on a well-structured codebase with clear conventions and solid CI pipelines will get compounding productivity gains. The same tools on a legacy monolith with no tests and unclear ownership? You just generate technical debt at machine speed. The organizational version of this is exactly what McKinsey is describing.

- **Growth requires conviction during uncertainty.** The five companies profiled in the growth report didn't wait for clarity — they invested when the path forward was unclear. That takes organizational courage that most companies simply don't have when quarterly earnings pressure hits.

- **Complexity is the real enemy.** Not market conditions, not competition, not even technology limitations. The biggest barrier is internal complexity — duplicated processes, siloed decisions, bureaucratic inertia. The companies that win are the ones that ruthlessly simplify.

- **Diversification beats concentration.** Building multiple growth engines rather than betting on one thing is a consistent pattern. This is true at the company level and, I'd argue, equally true at the individual career level — engineers who build diverse skills across backend, infrastructure, and product thinking create their own "portfolio of growth engines" that compounds over time.
